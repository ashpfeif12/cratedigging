# The Setlist Craft Guide
### A House / Tech House DJ's Reference for Building Journeys

---

## Part 1: The Mindset

A DJ set is not a playlist. It's a **contract with the crowd**: *I will keep you dancing, and I will take you somewhere*. Every decision — track choice, order, transition style, energy shift — serves that contract.

The single biggest mistake bedroom DJs make: treating a set as "good songs in a row." Great sets have **architecture**. You're not picking tracks; you're placing chapters in a story.

The three-act dramatic arc (setup → tension → climax → resolution) has been used in Western storytelling for centuries. It works on dancefloors for the same reason it works in Jaws: humans are wired for rising tension and payoff. Your set is Jaws. The drop is the shark.

---

## Part 2: The Five Phases of a Club Set (1–2 hours)

Think in chapters, not tracks. For a standard 1–2 hour House / Tech House set, here's the blueprint:

### Phase 1 — The Invitation (first 10–15 min)
**Purpose:** Earn trust. Set the room's tone. Establish the pocket.
**Energy:** 60–70% of your eventual peak.
**BPM range:** 122–125
**Feel:** Groovy, rolling, spacious. Let basslines breathe. Avoid heavy vocals or big drops.
**What you're doing:** Showing the crowd you know what you're doing *without* asking for anything from them yet.

### Phase 2 — The Build (15–40 min)
**Purpose:** Raise the stakes. Introduce tension.
**Energy:** 70–80%.
**BPM range:** 124–127
**Feel:** Tighter drums, more insistent basslines, first hints of melody or vocal hooks. The shoulders start moving. Heads start nodding harder.
**What you're doing:** Graduating energy. Every 2–3 tracks should feel *slightly* more urgent than the last.

### Phase 3 — The Peak (40–80 min)
**Purpose:** Deliver the payoff. This is your Jaws finale.
**Energy:** 90–100%.
**BPM range:** 126–130
**Feel:** Your biggest tracks. Vocal hooks, recognizable drops, the records that made you love this music. 3–5 tracks in a **tight, high-chemistry sequence** — they should feel inevitable back-to-back.
**What you're doing:** Cashing the check. The crowd earned this. Give it to them.

### Phase 4 — The Reset (80–100 min)
**Purpose:** Release tension so you can build it again (or land the plane).
**Energy:** 75–85%.
**BPM range:** 124–127
**Feel:** A groovier, less vocal record. A stripped-back roller. You're pulling back to let the crowd breathe — *and* to create room for one more peak if you have it.
**What you're doing:** Proving you're not a one-trick pony. Two smaller peaks beat one flat plateau.

### Phase 5 — The Send-Off (last 5–15 min)
**Purpose:** Leave them with something that stays.
**Energy:** 70–80%, descending.
**BPM range:** 122–126
**Feel:** A track that *means* something. A classic, an emotional record, a vocal that lingers. The last thing they hear is the thing they'll remember.
**What you're doing:** Closing the loop. Giving the story an ending.

## Part 3: The Anchor-First Planning Method

Professional DJs don't build sets linearly. They build them around **anchors**.

**Step 1: Pick your anchor track.** This is the one record you *know* you're playing. Usually your biggest, most personal, or most crowd-tested cut. This lives at your peak (around the 60–70% mark of your set).

**Step 2: Plan your peak sequence.** 3–5 tracks around the anchor that stack chemistry. These should share key compatibility, BPM proximity, and emotional direction.

**Step 3: Plan your opener.** Work *down* from your peak. At 60–70% of peak energy. This sets your starting BPM and key.

**Step 4: Fill the path.** Opener → peak, progressing BPM and energy gradually, moving sensibly around the Camelot wheel.

**Step 5: Plan your landing.** 3–4 tracks of controlled descent from peak.

**Step 6: Prepare 20–30% more tracks than you need.** You need alternates. The crowd might not respond how you expect. Always have a Plan B record in each phase.

## Part 4: Harmonic Mixing — The Camelot Wheel

Rekordbox labels every track with a Camelot code (like 8A, 11B). The wheel is a map of which keys play nicely together.

**The 4 safe moves** from any starting key (example: 8A):

1. **Same key** (8A → 8A) — Perfect blend. Use for long layered transitions.
2. **±1 step same letter** (8A → 7A or 9A) — Smooth, subtle energy shift. The workhorse move.
3. **Same number, switch letter** (8A → 8B) — Mood flip. Minor → major lifts and brightens. Major → minor darkens and drives. This is the single most powerful emotional tool you have.
4. **+7 jump** (8A → 3A or use with care) — The "energy boost." Creates a dramatic key lift — a hands-in-the-air moment. Use sparingly; do it quickly (short blend) because melodies will clash over long transitions.

**The golden rule:** Key is one dimension. Energy is another. BPM is a third. A harmonically perfect transition with a 20 BPM gap still sounds terrible. Always think: **key + BPM + energy + mood** together.

**When key matters less:** Percussive tracks with little melodic content. Cutting quickly instead of long blends. Tracks with breakdowns where you're mixing over the breakdown.

**When key matters most:** Long melodic layered transitions, vocal tracks, tracks with strong synths or chord progressions.

## Part 5: BPM Strategy for House / Tech House

A 1–2 hour set should move roughly 122 → 128 BPM, but not linearly.

- **Don't do +1 BPM every track.** That's predictable and mechanical.
- **Cluster in zones.** Spend 15–20 min at 124, move to 126 for the build, sit at 127–128 through the peak.
- **Plateaus are powerful.** Holding BPM while shifting energy via track selection is a pro move.
- **Small drops are fine.** Going 128 → 126 at the reset is valid — the energy drops without feeling like a crash because the groove continues.

---

## Part 6: The Vocal Rule

Vocals are the most memorable element in a track. They also clash the hardest.

- **Never layer two vocal tracks** unless you're doing a specific acapella-over-instrumental trick.
- **Plan your vocal moments.** 3–5 vocal highlights across a 90-min set. Space them out — a vocal every track is exhausting.
- **Save your biggest vocal for the peak or the send-off.** It's your emotional payload. Don't waste it at minute 20.

1. Search a DJ you love (e.g., "Cloonee Resistance 2024")
2. Open a recent set with full tracklist
3. Note the **first 5 tracks** (their opener formula) and the **tracks around the hour mark** (their peak material)

5. Check "Most Played" weekly charts on 1001tracklists Tech House genre page for current meta.

### The 2-3x rule
For a 90-minute set (~20 tracks), build a crate of 50–60 candidates. You want options, not a locked sequence.

## Part 8: Tagging — How to Actually Find Tracks When You Need Them

In Rekordbox, tag every track with:

- **Phase tag:** Opener / Builder / Peak / Reset / Closer / Tool
- **Energy rating:** 1–10 (use MyTag or comments)
- **Vocal:** Full vocal / Hook / Instrumental
- **Mood:** Dark, Groovy, Euphoric, Funky, Driving, Melodic
- **Use case:** "Opens cold," "Works after vocal," "Long breakdown at 2:30"

When you're building a set, filter by Phase + BPM range + Camelot-compatible key. This turns a 500-track library into a 12-track shortlist in seconds.

## Part 9: The Repeatable Process (The Setlist Workflow)

Every time you prep a set, run this playbook:

1. **Define the gig.** Venue, crowd, time slot, set length, what DJ plays before/after you. Shorter sets = narrower BPM range, tighter theme.
2. **Choose a theme.** "Late-night groove," "Peak-time tech house," "Sunset warm-up." One sentence.
3. **Pick your anchor.** The ONE track you know you're playing.
4. **Build the peak sequence** (3–5 tracks around the anchor).
5. **Choose your opener** (60–70% of peak energy).
6. **Fill the arc** (opener → peak, then peak → close).
7. **Check harmonic flow** on the Camelot wheel. Flag any risky transitions.
8. **Check BPM flow.** No jarring jumps unless intentional.
9. **Pick 20–30% alternates** for each phase.
10. **Rehearse transitions** — especially the peak sequence. Those 3–5 tracks need to be surgical.

## Part 10: The 10 Mistakes That Kill Sets

1. **Opening too hard.** You have nowhere to go.
2. **Flat energy for too long.** Crowds need contrast.
3. **All killer, no filler.** Without valleys, peaks feel smaller.
4. **Vocal-stacking.** Two vocal tracks layered = mud.
5. **BPM jumps of >3 without a reset track.** Jarring.
6. **Ignoring key entirely.** Even one clash per set is audible.
7. **Only mixing in one key for 90 minutes.** Sterile and repetitive.
8. **Not preparing alternates.** When plan A fails, you freeze.
9. **Rehearsing individual tracks, not transitions.** The transitions are the set.
10. **Forgetting the ending.** A set without a closer fizzles instead of finishes.
